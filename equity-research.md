### #1 — Visa (V) | Conviction: 88/100

**What It Does**: Visa operates the world's largest payment network. It does not lend money or take credit risk. Instead, it charges a small toll — roughly 0.1–0.2% per transaction — every time a Visa card is swiped, tapped, or clicked anywhere on earth. In 2025, Visa processed over $15 trillion in payment volume.

**Why It's Hard to Compete Against**

The Visa network has been built over 60 years. To displace it, a competitor would need:
1. Every bank in every country to issue their card instead of Visa
2. Every merchant in every country to install their terminal and accept their card
3. Consumers worldwide to switch cards

This is a true two-sided network effect: more issuers → more cardholders → more merchants accept → more issuers are motivated to issue. The network becomes more valuable with every participant. Mastercard is the only viable alternative, and together they form a duopoly that controls ~90%+ of global card transactions outside China.

| ROIC | **54.32%** — exceptional; barely any capital is needed to operate |
| Net Margin | **51.68%** — one of the highest of any company on earth |

| P/E (trailing / forward) | 28.53x / **23.48x** |
| EV/EBITDA | 20.79x |
| EV/FCF | 29.58x |
| Revenue Growth (5Y avg) | ~10–12% |

**Moat Ratings**

| Distribution | **10/10** | Embedded in every bank system, every POS terminal globally |
| Patents/IP | **7/10** | Tokenization, fraud models, processing — but moat is network, not patents |

**Management**

Ryan McInerney (CEO since 2023): Smart capital allocator. The company consistently buys back ~$15–17B in shares per year. Dividend growing. No empire-building acquisitions. Plaid acquisition attempt failed (DOJ blocked) — which was probably correct; Visa doesn't need fintech acquisitions. Management understands that their business is running a toll booth and does not try to overcomplicate it.

**Why Buy This Instead of VOO?**

VOO gives you ~3% Visa exposure at 500-stock dilution. Buying V directly gives you concentrated exposure to a business compounding at 10–12% annually with 54% ROIC and 51% net margins. In 20 years, Visa will process materially more of the world's payments as cash continues to decline globally (still ~40% of consumer transactions worldwide). The compound at these economics beats the S&P 500 in the long run even at a "fair" starting price.

**Valuation**

Forward P/E of 23.48x for a business that earns 54% ROIC with 51% net margins and 10%+ growth is not expensive. The fair value multiple is arguably 30–35x, implying the stock is modestly undervalued or fairly priced. Not a screaming bargain, but a business you can hold forever.

**Risks**

- **Regulatory/antitrust**: DOJ filed antitrust suit over debit card routing practices (2024). A forced structural remedy could affect interchange economics
- **Crypto/CBDC**: Central bank digital currencies could, in theory, bypass Visa over a 15–20 year horizon. This is the primary long-term tail risk
- **Stablecoins**: Visa is adapting (processes stablecoin settlements on Solana). But disruption risk is real at the margin
- **Competition**: RuPay (India), UnionPay (China), and regional schemes expanding. Not material to global earnings yet

### #3 — Constellation Software (CSU.TO) | Conviction: 86/100

**What It Does**: Constellation Software acquires vertical market software (VMS) companies — niche, mission-critical software for specific industries (funeral home directors, municipal water utilities, dental practice management, marina operators, etc.). Each business serves 50–2,000 customers and has 90%+ retention because switching is operationally catastrophic.

**Why This Is Special**

This is the closest public-market analog to a private equity compounder with the benefit of permanent capital and public-market discipline. Mark Leonard's model:
1. Identify VMS businesses with monopoly-like positions in their niches
2. Pay 1–2× revenues (reasonable valuations because these are boring businesses)
3. Never sell (hold forever → earnings compound; no transaction costs or fund cycles)
4. Decentralize: leave management teams in place, just provide capital and best practices
5. Reinvest all FCF into more acquisitions

Since going public in 2006, CSU has compounded at ~30% annually. The TAM for VMS acquisitions globally is enormous — there are estimated 100,000+ VMS businesses worldwide still in private hands. CSU has acquired ~900 and shows no sign of running out of targets.

The acquisition team, operating group presidents, and decentralized model don't depend on Leonard personally being on the board. This is a process, not a person — the best kind of succession risk.

Jeff Bender and the operating group presidents have run the business day-to-day for years. The recent Topicus.com and Lumine Group spin-offs show the model is replicating itself.

### #4 — Copart (CPRT) | Conviction: 85/100

**What It Does**: Copart is an online vehicle auction platform. When an insurance company totals a vehicle, Copart handles the entire disposition — transport to one of 250+ storage yards, titling, and online auction to registered buyers in 185+ countries. Copart earns a service fee; it does not take inventory risk.

**Why It's Hard to Compete Against**

Three mutually reinforcing barriers:

1. **Insurer supply relationships**: 75–80% of vehicles come from State Farm, Progressive, Allstate, GEICO, etc. These relationships are embedded in insurer systems and took decades to build.
2. **Land footprint**: 250+ storage yards across 11 countries. Urban land in major metro markets is effectively impossible to replicate at scale — you cannot buy 50 acres near Los Angeles.
3. **Global buyer liquidity**: ~1 million registered buyers in 185+ countries. More buyers → better auction prices → insurers earn more → insurers won't leave Copart.

IAA (acquired by Ritchie Bros.) has existed for decades and cannot close the gap on buyer depth. The flywheel continues to widen.

**Structural Tailwind**: Total-loss rates hit a record 22.8% in 2025 as repair costs on modern vehicles (cameras, sensors, advanced composites) have soared. Insurance companies total more cars now than at any point in history. This is structural.

**Why Buy Instead of VOO?**

Copart occupies a structural position: it processes the inevitable byproduct of an increasingly expensive vehicle fleet. As vehicles get more complex and expensive to repair, the total-loss rate rises, benefiting Copart. It's a business that gets stronger during economic downturns (insurers total more, not fewer, cars when margins are squeezed). The FCF yield of 5.15% is offering you an inflation-beating return even if growth were to stop entirely — which it won't.

**Current Opportunity**

The stock fell 20–30% from highs because:

### #7 — Moody's Corporation (MCO) | Conviction: 82/100

**What It Does**: Moody's operates two business segments: Moody's Investors Service (MIS, the credit ratings agency) and Moody's Analytics (MA, risk data and SaaS). MIS rates bonds issued by corporations, municipalities, and sovereign governments. Every major bond offering globally requires a credit rating, and issuers must pay Moody's.

**Why the Rating Agency Business Is Special**

The credit rating business is a government-created oligopoly. To have ratings recognized by banks, insurers, and pension funds for regulatory capital purposes, an agency must be an NRSRO (Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organization) — a designation the SEC grants sparingly. There are 10 NRSROs, but only three matter: Moody's (~34% share), S&P (~40%), and Fitch (~16%). Regulation created the moat; it doesn't matter how good a new competitor's ratings are if they're not recognized for regulatory purposes.

The business model is toll-booth: every bond issue pays a rating fee. When debt markets are active, revenues spike. When they slow, revenues dip — it's somewhat cyclical. But over 30-year timescales, global debt issuance grows with GDP plus financial deepening.

**Moody's vs. S&P Global**

MCO is the superior investment over SPGI at current levels:
- MCO ROIC: ~22–27% (consistent; business not impaired)
- SPGI ROIC: 9.25% (collapsed post-IHS Markit acquisition)
- MCO also has Moody's Analytics growing ~10%+ annually (subscription-based risk data), which smooths the cyclicality of ratings

**Financials**

### #8 — Ferrari (RACE) | Conviction: 80/100

**What It Does**: Ferrari manufactures and sells high-performance sports and GT cars. It deliberately limits production — 13,640 units shipped in 2025 — to maintain artificial scarcity and protect brand desirability. A new Ferrari has a 2-year waiting list. Secondary market prices are typically 1.5–4× the retail price for new models.

**Why Ferrari Is a Luxury Monopoly**

Ferrari is not an auto company — it's a luxury brand that happens to make cars. The economics are closer to Hermès than to BMW:
- Margins **expand** as production grows only slightly (personalization/options revenue)
- Price increases are absorbed without demand destruction (Veblen good)
- The customer base is multi-generational; Ferrari buyers have typically owned several
- The exclusivity is the product; selling more would destroy the brand

**Financials**

| Metric | 2025 |
|---|---|
| Revenue | €7.146B (+7%) |
| EBIT Margin | **29.5%** |
| EBITDA Margin | **38.8%** |
| Industrial FCF | €1.538B (+50% YoY!) |
| Units Shipped | 13,640 |
| Order Book | Through end of 2027 (fully subscribed!) |

**Why Buy Instead of VOO?**

Ferrari has arguably the only consumer brand moat comparable to Hermès. The 2-year order book means revenue visibility is exceptional. The move into personalization (each car is customized; average transaction price rising) and special/limited series (Icona, XX program) expands margins without volume. Coming EV Ferrari models will sell immediately — the waiting list will expand, not contract. This is a rare business that is structurally immune to the pricing pressure facing most consumer companies.

**The Valuation Question**

Ferrari trades at ~45–55x P/E — expensive. But:
- Industrial FCF of €1.538B growing 50% YoY at €7B revenue
- Order book guarantees 2027 revenue
- Personalization revenue expanding margins
- EV transition is a tailwind, not headwind (scarcity + technology = premium)

The honest conclusion: Ferrari at 45–55x is not a bargain. It's fair-to-full price for an extraordinary business. You pay a quality premium. The expected return is lower than other names on this list but the *risk* is also lower — this business is not going anywhere.

**Risks**

- **EV transition complexity**: Designing electric Ferraris at small volumes requires enormous R&D. Battery fires are brand-damaging. This is a genuine technical challenge
- **China softness**: Ultra-luxury sentiment in China has been muted since 2023; China is a meaningful market for Ferrari
- **Succession**: John Elkann (Agnelli family) controls Ferrari. Benedetto Vigna (CEO) has been excellent. Any disruption to Vigna's leadership would be notable
